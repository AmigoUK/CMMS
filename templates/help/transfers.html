Part Transfers

Each site is its own budget account, with its own parts inventory. When one site needs a part another site has in surplus, you don't have to order a new one — you transfer it. Every transfer is recorded with two sides (source out, destination in) and an audit trail.

Who can do what? A supervisor at the source site requests a transfer. A supervisor at the destination site approves it. Admins can do both. The two-sided gate prevents one site silently pulling stock from another.
{# ── Transfer Lifecycle ─────────────────────────────────────── #}
Transfer Lifecycle
Pending Completed
Or, at any pending stage: Cancelled
  • Pending — Source supervisor requested. No stock has moved yet. Source supervisor or destination supervisor can still cancel.
  • Completed — Destination supervisor approved. Stock decremented at source, incremented (or created) at destination, atomically. Two linked stock adjustments are written for audit.
  • Cancelled — No stock movement, just a record of the request and cancellation reason.
{# ── How to request ─────────────────────────────────────────── #}
Requesting a Transfer
  1. Switch to the source site using the navbar site switcher.
  2. Open the part in Inventory → Parts. Click the Request Transfer button (only visible when the part has stock on hand).
  3. Pick the destination site (your accessible sites only — admins see all).
  4. Enter the quantity (must not exceed source on-hand) and an optional note.
  5. Submit. The transfer becomes Pending.

The unit cost on the source part is captured at request time, so the transfer's value stays correct even if costs change later.

{# ── How to approve ─────────────────────────────────────────── #}
Approving a Transfer

Pending transfers waiting for your approval are surfaced in two places when you log in as the destination-site supervisor:

  • The Inventory navbar item shows a counter badge for parts low on stock plus pending transfers.
  • The Dashboard shows a "Transfers awaiting your approval" card.

Click into the transfer detail. Verify the part, quantity, and source. Click Approve & Complete. Stock moves immediately. If the destination site doesn't yet have this part (matched by part number), it is created with the same catalogue info and the transferred quantity.

{# ── Cross-site surplus on the reorder report ───────────────── #}
Cross-Site Surplus on Reorder

On the Inventory → Reorder Report page, when a part is below minimum stock and another site is sitting on more than its own maximum, you'll see a green "Cross-site surplus" banner with one-click "Request Transfer" links. Use these before placing a purchase order — you save money and reduce overall inventory waste.

The same banner accounts for any pending inbound transfers, so the shortfall shown is the net shortfall after expected receipts.

{# ── Cancellation ───────────────────────────────────────────── #}
Cancelling a Pending Transfer

Either the original requester, a supervisor at the source site, or a supervisor at the destination site (and admins) can cancel a transfer while it's still pending. Provide a short reason — it's shown on the transfer detail and audit log. Completed transfers cannot be cancelled — to undo an already-moved transfer, request a new transfer in the opposite direction.

{# ── Common questions ──────────────────────────────────────── #}
Common Questions
What if the destination site doesn't have the part yet?
It's created automatically on approval, copying the catalogue info (name, description, supplier, unit cost) from the source and starting at the transferred quantity.
Can two transfers race to drain the same source?
No — the approval step takes a row-level lock on the source part, re-checks stock sufficiency under the lock, and rolls back if there isn't enough.
Where does this show up in spend reports?
On Reports → Spend Overview the source site sees the value as Transfers out (positive spend), and the destination site sees the same value as Transfers in (negative spend / inflow). Net inter-site movement is zero organisationally; per-site it's a cost movement.
